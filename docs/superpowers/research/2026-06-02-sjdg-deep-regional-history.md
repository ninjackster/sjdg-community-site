# Deep Regional History — The Land Where San José de Gracia, Jalisco Sits

**Compiled 2026-06-02 for the SJdG collective-history / La Crónica project.**
A companion to `2026-06-02-sjdg-history-dossier.md` (which covers the local post-1700 story).
This dossier reaches as far back as the archaeological and ethnohistoric record allows for the
**region**, then bridges forward to the founding of "El Bramido."

## Disambiguation (load-bearing)

The subject is the **delegación / comisaría of San José de Gracia inside the municipality of
Tepatitlán de Morelos**, in the **Altos Sur / Los Altos de Jalisco** region of **Jalisco, Mexico**
(INEGI locality 140930291; ~95 km NE of Guadalajara; upper Río Verde / Lerma–Santiago drainage). It is
**NOT** San José de Gracia, Michoacán, nor the towns of the same name in Aguascalientes/Sinaloa. No
deep-history source is locality-specific for a small ranchero town, so the research is scoped to the
**region** and every major claim is tagged:

- **[locality]** — the SJdG townsite itself
- **[Tepatitlán/Altos Sur]** — the Tepatitlán micro-zone / Altos Sur
- **[regional/Nueva Galicia]** — Los Altos broadly / the Nueva Galicia frontier

Confidence: **High / Med / Low**. Contradictions are flagged inline.

## 1. Pre-ceramic / Archaic & earliest peopling

- **[regional/Nueva Galicia] — Med/Low.** There is **no preceramic or Archaic site documented at or
  immediately around Tepatitlán / the Altos Sur**; deep human-antiquity evidence for central-western
  Mexico comes from neighboring zones, not Los Altos itself. Treat the earliest layer as *regional
  backdrop*, not local fact.
- **[regional/Nueva Galicia] — Med.** Mexico's broader Pleistocene/Paleo-Indian record is firm:
  El Cedral (San Luis Potosí, the next state NE), Valsequillo (Puebla) and Babisuri (BCS) carry
  occupation arguments back ~30,000–40,000 years; **Chiquihuite Cave** (central-northern Mexico)
  yields cultural evidence dated to the Last Glacial Maximum (~26,500–19,000 BP), with claims pushing
  dispersal to ~33,000–31,000 BP (Ardelean et al., *Nature*, 2020). *Source:* Nature 2020,
  s41586-020-2509-0; "Late Pleistocene Human Occupation of Mexico" (ResearchGate). These are far from
  the Altos but establish that the central-western highlands were within the peopled world by the end
  of the Pleistocene.
- **[regional/Nueva Galicia] — Med.** For the El Bajío / Lerma corridor (immediately SE of the Altos),
  central Mexico is described as first settled c. **8000–7000 BCE**, with the Lerma River as an early
  migration/travel corridor that later fed the first settlement of Chupícuaro. *Source:* Miami Univ.
  Anthropology 3-D Museum, "Chupícuaro — History Overview"; Chupícuaro (Wikipedia). **Confidence note:**
  the "8000–7000 BCE" figure is a general statement about *central* Mexico, applied here only as
  regional context, not as evidence of Archaic occupation in the Altos.

**Bottom line for the timeline:** the honest earliest anchor for the *region* is "highlands of
central-western Mexico peopled by the terminal Pleistocene / early Holocene (≈10,000+ BCE), regional
context only — no documented site in the Altos." Everything firmer starts in the ceramic era.

## 2. Pre-Hispanic archaeology & cultures (Río Verde / Altos de Jalisco zone)

### Chupícuaro & the El Bajío sphere (the SE neighbor)
- **[regional/Nueva Galicia] — High.** The **Chupícuaro** culture (Acámbaro valley, Guanajuato, on the
  Lerma) flourished in the Middle–Late Preclassic, dated variously **c. 800/600 BCE – c. 200/300 CE**.
  Its ceramic style spread widely; "similar pottery has been found around the Yuriria and Cuitzeo lake
  basins, **in parts of Jalisco**, and throughout the Bajío," indicating strong exchange across the
  Lerma–Bajío zone that abuts the Altos on the SE. *Source:* Chupícuaro (Wikipedia); INAH Lugares
  ("El Opeño, Chupícuaro…"); openedition/nda "Chupícuaro." This is the most plausible Preclassic
  cultural influence reaching toward the Altos, but **no Chupícuaro site is documented at Tepatitlán**
  — link it as *sphere of influence*, not occupation. **Conf. of the direct Tepatitlán tie: Low.**

### The Teuchitlán tradition / guachimontones (the W neighbor)
- **[regional/Nueva Galicia] — High.** The **Teuchitlán tradition**, defined largely by **Phil C.
  Weigand** (and his wife Acelia García de Weigand) from the early 1970s, is the great archaeological
  culture of west-central Jalisco. It flourished **c. 350 BCE – 450/500 CE** (Late Formative through
  Classic), with its monumental **guachimontones** (concentric circular plazas + stepped central altar)
  and shaft-and-chamber tombs. The largest site, **Los Guachimontones** at Teuchitlán, sits in the
  **Tequila Valleys** near the Tequila Volcano — the cultural *core*. *Source:* Teuchitlán culture
  (Wikipedia); INAH Lugares "Guachimontones"; Weigand historiography in Esparza López, *Relaciones*
  (El Colegio de Michoacán), redalyc 13748262004 / scielo S0185-39292016000400063.
- **[regional/Nueva Galicia] — Med.** Reach beyond the core: guachimonton-type architecture appears as
  far as Comala (Colima), La Gloria (Guanajuato), and Bolaños (Jalisco). Beekman's model frames
  Teuchitlán as a hierarchy where elite control thins toward the periphery (a "segmentary"/flexible
  ritual frontier per Weigand). *Source:* Teuchitlán culture (Wikipedia); Frontiers in Political
  Science 2022 ("The Collapse of a Collective Society").
- **[Tepatitlán/Altos Sur] — Low (important caveat).** The published Teuchitlán core is the Tequila
  Valleys to the **west/southwest** of Guadalajara; **Los Altos (Tepatitlán, the upper Río Verde) is
  east of that core and is best characterized as a sparsely-settled eastern margin/frontier**, not a
  monumental center. Searches for an explicit "Teuchitlán reaches the Río Verde / the Altos" statement
  in Weigand did **not** surface a firm claim — so any guachimonton presence at Tepatitlán is
  **unconfirmed** and should be presented as "the Altos lay on the eastern edge of the west-Mexican
  Formative–Classic world, between the Teuchitlán (W) and Chupícuaro/Bajío (SE) spheres."

### Summary of the archaeological picture
- **[Tepatitlán/Altos Sur] — Med.** The Altos were a **thinly populated frontier/interface zone** for
  most of pre-Hispanic time — never a culture core like the Tequila Valleys or the Bajío lake basins.
  This is consistent both with the archaeology (no major monumental site reported at Tepatitlán) and
  with the 16th-century ethnohistory (dispersed agriculturalists, contested by nomads to the NE).

## 3. Indigenous peoples at contact (16th c.)

### Who held the Tepatitlán micro-region — and resolving the confusion
- **[Tepatitlán/Altos Sur] — High.** The dominant scholarship (John P. Schmal; Carolyn Baus de Czitrom;
  *Indigenous Mexico* / MexConnect syntheses) places the **Tecuexes** as the indigenous group holding
  the **Tepatitlán–Jalostotitlán–Mexticacán–Valle de Guadalupe–Yahualica** belt at contact. The
  Tecuexes occupied northern Jalisco "from Magdalena and Tequila in the west to Jalostotitlán and Cerro
  Gordo in the east," their eastern range reaching the **northwestern Altos** and explicitly including
  **Tepatitlán**. *Source:* Schmal, "Indigenous Jalisco in the Sixteenth Century" (indigenousmexico.org);
  Tecuexe (Wikipedia, citing Baus de Czitrom).
- **[Tepatitlán/Altos Sur] — High (lifeways).** Tecuexes were **sedentary agriculturalists** ("liked to
  make their houses in valleys and gorges near rivers"), growing maize and beans, working as artisans/
  carpenters, and domesticating agave for pulque — but with a strong **warrior** reputation. *Source:*
  Tecuexe (Wikipedia).
- **[regional/Nueva Galicia] — Med (language).** Tecuexe is extinct and classed as **Uto-Aztecan**,
  likely related to Nahua (Corachol-branch locative suffixes -íc/-tíc paralleling Nahua -tlan). Some
  colonial Tecuexes wrote in Nahuatl; it is unsettled whether Nahuatl was native or a lingua franca
  (Nahuatl spread as a regional lingua franca after the conquest). *Source:* Tecuexe (Wikipedia);
  Schmal (indigenousmexico.org). **Contradiction flag:** older popular sources loosely call all these
  groups "Chichimeca-speaking" semi-nomads (e.g., Grokipedia "Altos de Jalisco"); the more careful
  ethnohistory treats Tecuexes as sedentary Uto-Aztecan farmers, distinct from the truly nomadic
  Guachichiles. Prefer the latter.

### The Tecuexe / Guachichile overlap at Tepatitlán (explicit contradiction)
- **[Tepatitlán/Altos Sur] — Med, FLAGGED.** Several syntheses say the **Guachichiles** (a genuinely
  nomadic Chichimeca people of the NE) ranged "near Lagos de Moreno, Arandas, Ayo el Chico, **and
  Tepatitlán**," and that the Tecuexes "fought with the hostile Guachichiles in the eastern reaches."
  *Source:* Schmal (indigenousmexico.org). **Resolution:** this is not a true contradiction but a
  **frontier overlap** — Tepatitlán sat on the *eastern edge of settled Tecuexe country*, abutting/
  contested by Guachichile nomads pressing in from the NE. Best phrasing: *"At contact the Tepatitlán
  micro-region was held by the sedentary, Uto-Aztecan-speaking **Tecuexes**, on the frontier with the
  nomadic **Guachichiles** to the northeast."*

### The other regional players
- **[regional/Nueva Galicia] — High.** **Caxcanes (Cazcanes)** — partly nomadic, more politically
  organized — held the northern zone with religious/population centers at **Teul, Tlaltenango,
  Juchipila, Nochistlán (Zac.) and Teocaltiche (Jal.)**, NW/N of the Tecuexes. Tradition holds the
  Caxcanes earlier intruded into Tecuexe land; the two groups were intermittently hostile for
  generations before contact. *Source:* Schmal; MexConnect; Mixtón War (Wikipedia).
- **[regional/Nueva Galicia] — High.** **Guachichiles** and other **Chichimeca** nomads occupied the
  arid NE frontier (toward Zacatecas/San Luis Potosí) — the groups that would fight the Chichimeca War.
- **[regional/Nueva Galicia] — Med.** **Coca** speakers held the Guadalajara/Lake Chapala area south of
  the Tecuexes; c. 1530 the immediate Guadalajara-valley population (~1,000 dispersed farmers) spoke
  Tecuexe and Coca. *Source:* indigenousmexico.org.
- **[Tepatitlán/Altos Sur] — Low (folk claim).** The municipal history (tepatitlan.gob.mx; Mi Municipio)
  asserts the site was inhabited "primitively" by **Otomíes** (cave-dwelling hunters) *before* the
  Tecuexes. This is a **municipal/folk tradition**, not corroborated by academic ethnohistory — keep at
  Low and attribute. Tepatitlán = Nahuatl "place of hard stone" (or "place of the sacred knife").
  *Source:* tepatitlan.gob.mx/historia; ecured.

## 4. Spanish conquest of Nueva Galicia (Nuño de Guzmán, 1529–1531)

- **[regional/Nueva Galicia] — High.** **Nuño Beltrán de Guzmán** left Mexico City in **December 1529**
  with ~300–500 Spaniards and 8,000+ indigenous allies/Tarascan auxiliaries; entered **Tonalá on 25
  March 1530** and drove a brutal campaign (Feb–Jun 1530) north toward Zacatecas, conquering the lands
  that became Nueva Galicia. *Source:* Nuño de Guzmán (Wikipedia); indigenousmexico.org "Brutal Reign";
  Encyclopedia.com.
- **[Tepatitlán/Altos Sur] — High.** Guzmán dispatched captain **Pedro Almíndez Chirinos (Chirino)**
  from Cuitzeo in **1530** to reconnoiter the region up to Zacatecas — the entrada that first brought
  Spaniards through the future Tepatitlán. Franciscans (frailes Rogelio/Miguel de Bolonia-era
  evangelization; local tradition names Rea and Moreno) founded the first church to San Francisco,
  giving "San Francisco de Tepatitlán." *Source:* tepatitlan.gob.mx/historia; Mi Municipio.
  *(This 1530 Almíndez Chirinos entrada is the same anchor already in the companion dossier's Section D.)*
- **[regional/Nueva Galicia] — High.** **Reino de Nueva Galicia** named by royal decree **25 Jan 1531**.
  Guzmán ordered conquered Indians distributed into **encomiendas**; Altos communities (Tecuexe/Caxcan)
  were parceled to encomenderos for tribute/labor. *Source:* Nueva Galicia (Wikipedia); indigenousmexico.org.
- **[regional/Nueva Galicia] — High.** **Guadalajara was founded four times** before settling:
  1. **Nochistlán** (Caxcan country, Zac.) — 5 Jan 1532 (Juan de Oñate, by Guzmán's order)
  2. **Tonalá** — Aug 1533
  3. **Tlacotlán/Tlacotán** — 1535 (received city title + arms 1539)
  4. **Valle de Atemajac** — **14 Feb 1542** (final, present site; first cabildo, alcalde Miguel de Ibarra)
  *Source:* "Las cuatro fundaciones de Guadalajara" (Sociedad Genealógica y de Historia Familiar de
  México; jalisco.gob.mx). The instability that forced the moves was driven largely by Caxcan
  resistance — i.e., the run-up to the Mixtón War.

## 5. The Mixtón War (1540–1542)

- **[regional/Nueva Galicia] — High.** A **Caxcan-led uprising** ("heart and soul" of the revolt; allied
  Tecuexes, Cocas, and Chichimecas) aimed to expel the Spanish from NW Mexico, named for the
  **Peñol del Mixtón** hill fortress (Zac.). Triggered ~mid-1540 by the arrest/hanging of indigenous
  leaders and abuses by encomenderos; rebels killed encomenderos and priests and fortified the peñoles
  of Mixtón, Nochistlán, and Juchipila. Principal leader: **Tenamaztle (Tenamaxtli) of Nochistlán**.
  *Source:* Mixtón War (Wikipedia); Mexico Unexplained; indigenousmexico.org.
- **[regional/Nueva Galicia] — High.** **Pedro de Alvarado** attacked the Nochistlán peñol (24 Jun 1541),
  was repelled and crushed in the retreat, dying of his injuries in Guadalajara **4 Jul 1541**. Viceroy
  **Antonio de Mendoza** then led a massive force (~450 Spaniards + 30,000–60,000 Aztec/Tlaxcalan and
  other allies), took Nochistlán **9 Nov 1541**, and reduced **Mixtón in early 1542**, ending the war.
  *Source:* Mixtón War (Wikipedia).
- **[regional/Altos Sur] — High (consequences).** Aftermath was catastrophic for the indigenous Altos:
  "thousands were driven off in chains to the mines, and many of the survivors (mostly women and
  children) were transported … to work on Spanish farms and haciendas." Combined with epidemics, this
  depopulated and broke the Tecuexe/Caxcan social order across the Altos — clearing demographic space
  that Spanish/criollo ranchero settlement would later fill. *Source:* indigenousmexico.org; Tecuexe
  (Wikipedia).

## 6. The Chichimeca War (c. 1550–1590) & the criollo-ranchero repopulation

- **[regional/Nueva Galicia] — High.** The **Chichimeca War (1550–1590)** — sparked by the silver
  strikes at **Zacatecas (1546)** and the wagon roads crossing nomad land — was the longest, costliest
  Spanish–indigenous conflict in New Spain, fought against Guachichiles, Zacatecos, Guamares and others.
  The Altos sat on the strategic corridor between Zacatecas/Guanajuato mines and the core. *Source:*
  Chichimeca War (Wikipedia); Powell, *Soldiers, Indians and Silver* (the standard authority — cited
  here from secondary summaries; verify page refs against the book).
- **[regional/Nueva Galicia] — High (pacification turn).** After military "fire and blood" failed,
  Spain shifted to **"paz por compra" (peace by purchase)**: the 1584 Bishop-of-Guadalajara proposal
  for Christianized towns, formalized 1585 by the Audiencia — distributing food, cloth, tools, seed and
  livestock to Chichimeca leaders for oaths of peace and resettlement. *Source:* Chichimeca War
  (Wikipedia); Univ. of Arizona Press, *The Presidio and Militia… Vol. I* ("Doctor Orozco to the King").
- **[regional/Nueva Galicia] — High (Tlaxcalan colonists).** In **1591** Viceroy **Luis de Velasco**
  resettled ~**400 Tlaxcalan families** into eight frontier colonies as Christian exemplars/farmers,
  with privileges (land, tax exemption, right to bear arms). *Source:* Chichimeca War (Wikipedia);
  indigenousmexico.org.
- **[regional/Altos Sur] — Med/High (the ranchero turn).** With the indigenous population shattered
  (Mixtón) and the frontier pacified (1590s), the Altos were resettled chiefly by **Spanish/criollo
  families through the 16th–17th centuries** on dispersed *ranchos* and *estancias* — the
  Castilian/Basque/Andalusian/Galician stock that became the famous Altos ranchero/criollo heartland
  (recurring surnames González, Hernández, Padilla). "Most of the towns of the Altos were founded by
  Spanish families during the 16th and 17th centuries." *Source:* Altos de Jalisco (Wikipedia);
  companion dossier §B.
- **[locality] — Low/Med (bridge to El Bramido).** That ranchero-frontier process is the direct
  ancestor of San José de Gracia: at the **turn of the 19th century (commonly cited 1793; in tension
  with "took shape early 19th c.")** four **Hernández (Padilla)** brothers settled the lands of
  **"El Bramido,"** the nucleus that became SJdG. *(Detailed in the companion dossier §B/§D — not
  re-researched here; this section only supplies the deep-history bridge to it.)*
